Type I and Type II errors

Four possible outcomes of null hypothesis testing

our decision
H0 is NOT rejected H0 is rejected
the null hypothesis is really... true correct Type I error (false positive)
false Type II error (false negative) correct

Example: Covid tests

  • What's the null hypothesis?
    • Person doesn't have Covid
  • What's the alternative hypothesis?
    • Person has Covid
  • Two ways to go wrong
    • Test says positive when the person doesn't have Covid (Type I, false positive)
    • Test says negative when the person has Covid (Type II, false negative)
## One sample t-test

One sample t-test

The one sample t-test: is an unknown population mean different from a specific value?

  • Example: The average IQ is 100, by design. A professor wants to know whether the average IQ of students from university A is different from 100.
    • here, population = where we collect data = university A

When to use the one sample t-test

  • To compare a group of scores to a specified constant.
  • Scale variables (interval or ratio scores).
  • Population and sample are normally distributed.

One sample t-test example

Test whether students from university A have an average IQ which differ from 100.

  • Twenty students randomly selected from university A took the IQ test.
  • The IQ scores: 117 125 116 113 102 122 123 93 99 129 132 94 85 92 101 83 119 119 101 97 (mean = 108.1)
  • Null hypothesis: no difference between sample mean and 100. (i.e. H0: µ=100)
  • Alternative hypothesis: there is a difference between sample mean and 100. (i.e. H1: µ≠100)

How to test: the standard error of the mean

How confident are we that 108.1 represent the average IQ of all students from uni A (population mean)?

The standard error of the mean (SEM)

Imagine picking out 20 students randomly from uni A and calculating their mean

  • The value of the mean is likely to be different every time
  • Now do this 100 times, and construct a histogram of the mean value
  • The standard deviation of this distribution is the SEM

An animation: Sampling Distributions

Calculating the SEM from one experiment

  • We never do the experiment lots of times
  • But we can estimate the SEM from our one experiment
  • To calculate the SEM from one experiment:

    \[ SEM = \frac{s.d.}{\sqrt{n}} \]

One sample t-test: How many SEM units is our estimate from 100?

\[ t = \frac{\bar{x}-\mu}{SEM} \]

where

  • t is the value of the one-sample t—test
  • x bar is the mean of the sample (here 108.1)
  • μ is the number to be compared with (hypothetical mean*) (here, 100)
  • SEM is the standard error of the mean (here, 3.37)
  • so t = 2.4, in other words, our sample is 2.4 SEM units from the hypothetical mean.

Is 2.4 units good enough?

Is 2.4 units good enough?

  • Manually: consult the t table based on the degree of freedom (d.f. = n-1 = 20-1 = 19).
  • At alpha = 0.05, our t value is greater than the critical t value (2.4 > 2.093).
  • Thus, we reject H0, and say that uni A's students have significantly different IQ than 100. (And because 108.1 > 100, we say it's higher than 100.)

Reporting statistics

Statistical tests need to be reported once the data have been analysed (usually in a short paragraph)

Basic framework for the reporting:

  1. Statistical test that was performed
  2. The measures that were compared (with the different levels if there are any)
  3. The means and standard deviations
  4. Significant or non-significant?
  5. Specific test value – in this case the t-statistic (in later weeks, the F-value, X², r)
  6. Degrees of freedom – convention dictates that these are placed in rounded parentheses
  7. The specific p-value taken from R
  8. (confidence intervals/effect size)

Reporting statistics – One Sample t-Test

A one-sample t-test was run to determine whether the mean IQ scores for students in university A (n=20) was different from the average IQ (µ=100). Sampled IQ scores were normally distributed as assessed by Shapiro-Wilk’s test (p= 0.25) and there were no outliers in the data. The group of 20 students were found to have a significantly higher IQ (M=108.1, s.d.=15.05) than the average, t(19) = 2.4, p < 0.03.
